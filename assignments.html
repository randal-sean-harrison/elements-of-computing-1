Our Scenario

You and your fellow students are part of a newly formed data-analytics team for Innotech, a Chicago-based data analytics and marketing company which has been asked to recommend a plan to revitalize South Bend. Recently, an article in the Journal of Labor Economics has ranked South Bend as one of the least desirable places to live—its citizens the third most unhappy citizens in the United States. Curating Resources & Comprehension Checks
As each of you work throughout the semester, you will gather and share at least 1 resource per week that you deem useful to finding, analyzing, and conveying data impactfully, on paper or digitally.

I

Charts and Graphs
Learning to create charts and graphs involves learning which charts are best for which purpose (exploration vs. explanation), and how to avoid using unethical or misleading tactics in chart design. Your team will utilize these insights to produce charts for use in your slide deck.

II

Slide Deck
An impactful slide deck is more than just presenting raw data. You must make the data tell a story which engages and moves your audience to action. You'll learn to apply Gestalt theory to create powerful individual slides, and to apply narrative theory to assemble those slides into a powerful data story—built, in part, on those charts and graphs you made in the first assignment.

III

Slidedoc
An impactful presentation should not typically encourage deep perusal of the slides. To offer extended exploration of the data is the role of the slidedoc. We'll examine how to craft a strong slidedoc to accompany your slide deck.

IV

Data Dashboard
Some of the most interesting and useful storytelling takes place in real-time, interactive digital interfaces. You'll learn how to create a data dashboard in Tableau to enhance the way data can be used to make meaning on demand. The data dashboard will be a key part of your final presentation deliverables.

V

Website
Even a rudimentary understanding of how to create and upload a simple website will serve anyone in the data-* fields. While working toward your final presentation, we'll use Bootstrapr.io to craft a basic website to embed things like a data dashboard. We'll learn to upload our website to Github Pages to make it publicly available for free.

VI

Final Presentation
Applying the concepts you've learned this semester, you'll create and deliver an impactful final presentation, which is accompanied by a slidedoc and a data dashboard.

Comprehension Checks

Comprehension Checks are short quizzes due at the beginning of each week that has assigned readings from the course text books (except week 1). They assess your understanding of the readings for that week. They are meant to ensure you're keeping up with the all the new vocabulary and concepts in our readings, and to solidify and connect that knowledge to your work on previous and future assignments.

These are worth 15% of your course grade, so take the readings seriously. I will discard your lowest score in calculating your grade.

Charts and Graphs

For this assignment, each of you will complete 1 chart (embedded in a slide from either Powerpoint or Google Slides) using data you have obtained from your research. The chart-in-slide must be complete with all of the elements we have been discussing this semester. Any chart type listed on the Chart Chooser page is acceptable, except for "Big Single Number."

The data must be real and should reflect issues that you've discovered during your analysis. While you are not required to use these preliminary slides in your final presentation, and you can expect to improve them further as you improve throughout the semester, you should aim to create slides with real data that you would include in your final presentation.

Important
Each team member's chart must communicate a different insight/data story.

You'll accompany your chart with a written explanation of your design choices (design brief). You'll submit your finalized chart and design brief to me your own individual folders in our course Google drive.

My Recommended Approach
  • Meet in teams to do initial reading and searching for data sources. Agree on a general approach to the assignment and begin assigning readings and other duties.
  • Convene your group to brainstorm possible solutions. Aim for approximately 3-5 root causes of SB unhappiness indicated in the data.
  • Assign 1 chart per team member, each communicating a unique insight. Remember — creating data visualizations is as much to help you find meaning as your clients.

Slide Deck

Each team will submit a slide deck that will eventually become the slide deck for your final presentation. As a team you will need to make decisions about theme, tone, style, structure, etc.

View the assignment rubric.

For this assignment, your slide deck must:

  • Make best use of Gestalt design principles
  • Employ an impactful opener/closer
  • Support your claims with data-rich slides
  • Be structured as a sparkline (or "Hero's Journey") in which the audience is the hero

This assignment is graded collectively—each team member will receive the same grade, based on the quality of the slide deck. It is up to each team to determine how many slides long the slide deck is, and the way in which each member contributes to the slide deck is up to the group to decide.

This assignment should help you develop a strong first iteration of the presentation, a solid first version of your actual presentation. As such, it should include a storyline, a Big idea and supporting ideas, as well as the data to support those ideas. Ensure it's well-crafted and aesthetically appealing, applying the Gestalt theory you’ve been learning. The realer it is, the better the feedback I can give you in helping you to revise and expand for the final version in the final project. Certainly, you will continue to tweak your presentation toward the end of the semester, but this should be a fully realized first draft.

Slide Deck Length vs. Presentation Length
The number of slides is never a meaningful measure of presentation length, since one might give a 10 min presentation with six slides or sixty, depending on how many seconds the presenter spends on each slide.

You'll accompany your chart with a written explanation of your design choices (a 'design brief'), both of which you'll submit to me in a Google folder.

Slidedoc

Each team lead will submit a slidedoc on behalf of his or her team that will eventually become the slidedoc you hand in with your final presentation. The slidedoc is the place for extended research and analysis — the 'exploratory', rather than 'explanatory' space. As a team you will need to make decisions about theme, tone, style, structure, etc. Duarte's templates offer excellent guidance on how to structure and style the slidedoc.

View the assignment rubric.

For this assignment, your slide deck must:

  • Make best use of Gestalt design principles
  • Make best use of Duarte's suggested page templates.
  • Provide a deeper context and support your claims with annotated, data-rich charts and graphs
  • Each slidedoc should have the following elements:
    • Cover page
    • Table of contents
    • Executive Summary (expressing the big idea)
    • Slide listing the team members (with imagined titles)
    • Data-rich slides supporting your claims
    • Impactful images
    • Delineated sections
    • Summary and Recommendation slide
    • Resource/references slide

The slidedoc requires no design brief.

Your team lead will submit it on behalf of the team by uploading it to the appropriate Google folder.

Data Dashboard

Each team lead will submit a link to a data dashboard on behalf of his or her team that will eventually become the data dashboard you present in your final presentation. The power of a dashboard is the interconnectedness of the data, the way in which dashboard interface controls enable the audience to make new meaning on demand by adjusting values, but the point of a dashboard is to monitor data at-a-glance. You will use Tableau to create your dashboard and provide me the link to it live, when you submit. As a team you will need to work together to decide on which insights the dashboard will contain and how they are arranged.

View the assignment rubric.

For this assignment, your data dashboard must:

  • Make best use of Gestalt design principles
  • Offer simple insights (explanatory) which can also help the viewer make new meaning (exploratory), through the use of tooltips, etc.
  • Contain at least 4 insights/charts

The dashboard requires no design brief.

Each team lead must email the link to your public dashboard to your instructor to submit the assignment.

Website

The ability to create an impactful static website will serve you well in industry, where many people lack even basic web skills. We will work together during class periods to learn how to create an impactful static website and upload it to Github pages for free.

There is no formal assignment or deliverable for the website section of the course. While these are valuable skills, this section of the course is also designed to offer you extra time to prepare for your final presentation.

Final Presentation

Imagine the final presentation as a presentation to a small group of department heads from the South Bend city government. The city has contracted with your firm to perform data-intensive research on the question of how to make South Bend a happier place to live.

Your team will present your findings through several deliverables:

  • A formal presentation (and accompanying slide deck)
  • A slidedoc
  • A Tableau data dashboard
  • A design brief (see below)

Each team lead will submit these to their "Final Presentation Deliverables" folder on behalf of his or her team.

The final presentation requires a short design brief which describes only the difference between what you turned in earlier and what you're turning in for the final. Explain what you have revised and why.
